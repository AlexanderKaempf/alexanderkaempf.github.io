Tragic vs. depressive

December 12, 2025musicgeneral thoughts

This post is mainly the result of a recent discussion with a friend about what the most depressing piece of classical music is.

I'll start, just because it's interesting, with the etymology of the word tragic: It traces back to the Ancient Greek tragodia, which translates directly to "goat-song" (tragos + oide). Even the Ancient Greeks themselves were puzzled as to how this exactly came about; the most common explanation is that the prize for the best dramatic performance at Athenian festivals was often a goat. The person who influenced the meaning of the tragedy most, Aristotle, thought it was because the tragedy evolved from the dithyramb, a hymn sung in honour of Dionysus, containing a chorus of satyrs. But, whatever the exact origins are, the point is that the tragodia was always a dramatic imitation of a particular series of events, which was designed to arouse pity and fear among the audience, aiming to accomplish catharsis (purging/purification) of those emotions. Through the immense influence of Aristotles Poetics, these connotations (emphasis on the dramatic, performative, then cleansing aspect) have been carried through the ages to the modern day.

Contrast this with the etymology of "depressing": the latin deprimere. This means to press or crush down. Unlike the tragic, which in a certain sense is uplifting, through carthasis, the depressive leaves no light at the end of the tunnel. Depression is a psychological disorder, which is inherently unforgiving and has only recently become diagnosable and treatable. The tragic induces emotion in a way that makes it more manageable, the depressive crushes the mechanisms needed to process emotions and leaves its victim completely exposed to negative thought.

This is why I am confused when people often cite something the Tchaikovsky's sixth Symphony as being depressive. I can understand the train of thought, because Tchaikovsky struggled with depression his whole life and possibly intended his sixth Symphony to be a suicide note. The piece is also certainly extremely evocative of sorrowful emotions. But the tragic or depressive effect of something should be measured by its impact on (here) the listener, and not by the state the composer was in while writing the piece. Does Tchaikovsky's sixth really grind down the listeners psyche to a lasting, naked state of dejection, or does it temporarily induce melancholy in a way that makes the listener thoughtful, and more appreciative of the beautiful things in life? I think most people would experience the latter; this is central the the romantic temperament in general, and many parts of the symphony glimmer with hope and even convey joy.

An example of a genuinely depressive piece of music would, in my opinion, be Shostakovich's 15th Symphony. Instead of being heavily laden with tragic emotion, the first movement almost sounds whimsical; the composer described it as being "reminiscent of a toy store under a blue sky". But the movement has, for me, a cynical edge, seeming a bit empty and listless. The symphony quotes extensively from Rossini (William Tell Overture), Wagner (Tristan Chord, Fate motif), Glinka and Shostakovich's earlier works; the quotes seem mostly ironical and absurd due to seeming either too trivial and blase, too profound, or otherwise misplaced in the context they are used in. The most chilling part comes at the end: the symphony ends with an unsettling, jangly percussion beat, which sounds like the clattering of a skeleton, overlaid with an A major chord and a faraway melody on the celesta, fading of into nothing. Overall it feels drained and exhausted, like the composer has been intellectually beaten into submission after a lifetime of artistic suppression. The genuinely depressive effect comes from emotion being stifled and replaced by wistfulness/indifference/cynicism.

Anyway, I hope that didn't get too dark; it is just a distinction I think is worth talking about. Next post will be about something happier (like quantum information theory)!
