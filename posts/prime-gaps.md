The primes begin densely and thin out as we climb: $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, \dots$
What fascinates me is not the primes themselves but the **gaps** between them —
the sequence $g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n$.

## A deceptively simple question

How large can a gap be? How small? The prime number theorem tells us that, on
average, the gap near $x$ has size about $\log x$:

\[
g_n \approx \log p_n .
\]

But averages hide the drama. There are arbitrarily long stretches with no primes
at all, and — as the work on bounded gaps showed — there are infinitely many
pairs of primes that stay astonishingly close.

## Why I keep returning to it

What I find beautiful is the tension between **regularity in the average** and
**chaos in the detail**. The same object that obeys a clean asymptotic law
refuses, stubbornly, to be predictable up close. That tension is, to me, the
whole character of analytic number theory in miniature.

> A good problem is one whose answer reshapes the question.
